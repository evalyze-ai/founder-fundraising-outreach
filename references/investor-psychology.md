# Investor Psychology

Why investors actually reply, what they're scanning for, and how to write so you trip the right pattern-match. This is the layer beneath the templates: understand this and the templates make sense.

## The brutal truth about VC inboxes

A typical seed partner gets 50–200 cold inbound a week. They open the email, scan for 5 seconds, and decide one of three things:

1. **Reply** (very rare, maybe 1–2% of cold inbound)
2. **Skim and pass** (silently, ~30% read but don't reply)
3. **Delete unread** (the majority)

Your goal is to survive the 5-second scan. Everything in this skill is built around that constraint.

## What VCs scan for in 5 seconds

In rough order:

1. **Subject line:** does this match anything in my thesis?
2. **First line:** does this person know who I am or did they mass-blast?
3. **Two-line scan of the body:** is there a number? Is this a real company?
4. **Round size and stage:** does this match what I write checks for?
5. **Anything weird:** typos, em dashes, "world-class," NDA mention, PDF attachment

Fail any of these and the email is dead.

## Signal vs. noise

Investors have built strong signal-detection from seeing thousands of pitches. Here's what they actually weight.

### High-signal (get more of these)

- **Specific numbers in the first 3 lines.** $40K MRR is signal. "Strong growth" is noise.
- **A real customer name.** "Closed Acme Health Systems last month" is signal. "Major hospital network" is noise.
- **Honest acknowledgment of weaknesses.** "We have 8 months of retention data, too early to claim great retention but the early curve is X" is signal. Pretending data is more conclusive than it is is anti-signal.
- **Reference to something the partner specifically did or wrote.** Signal of research and intent.
- **Clear, specific use of funds.** "Two senior eng hires + 18 months runway to $1M ARR" is signal. "Build out the team and accelerate growth" is noise.
- **Customer language verbatim.** Quoting what a customer said about you, in their words, is signal.
- **A hard milestone with a date.** "We'll close our first 6-figure deal by end of Q1" is signal. "Strong pipeline" is noise.

### Low-signal (cut these)

- Adjectives without numbers
- "Revolutionary" / "disruptive" / "game-changing"
- Hockey-stick TAM with no math
- "We're not really competing with anyone"
- Press hits as proxy for traction
- LinkedIn endorsements
- Long bios with everything you've ever done
- Awards and certifications you've won
- "We've raised X from Y" (without specifics on what's the round and who Y actually is)

### Anti-signal (these actively hurt)

- NDA request
- PDF attachment over 5MB
- "We have no competitors"
- Personal Gmail address from the founder
- "Pre-revenue but the TAM is $100B"
- Generic "I admire your portfolio" personalization
- BCC of multiple investors
- Misspelling the firm or partner name
- Pitching for the fund AND their LPs in the first email
- "Quick coffee chat" with no context
- FOMO claims ("closing this week") that aren't true

## What VCs actually look for at each stage

### Pre-seed ($250K–$2M)

The bet is on the **founders and the insight**. Numbers are usually thin, so the weight shifts to:

- Why this team, why now, why this market
- Quality of the insight: what do you understand that the market doesn't yet
- Speed of learning: how fast do you iterate
- Early signal: design partners, LOIs, waitlist with engagement metrics, pilots
- Fundraise math: are you raising enough runway to hit a meaningful milestone

### Seed ($1M–$5M)

The bet is on the **early product-market-fit signal**. Investors want to see:

- Product in customer hands (not just demos)
- Some revenue or paid pilots, even if small
- 6–12 months of cohort data trending the right way
- A repeatable acquisition channel showing early signal
- The first few hires post-founders
- A clear path to Series A milestones (typically $1–3M ARR)

### Series A ($5M–$20M)

The bet is on **product-market fit being real and scalable**. Investors want:

- $1M–$3M ARR minimum (sector-dependent, lower for deeptech, higher for SaaS)
- Net Revenue Retention >100%
- 12+ months of cohort retention data
- Repeatable, attributable acquisition (not all referrals)
- Unit economics that work at the current scale, with a credible thesis on why they'll improve
- A management team forming under the founders (head of sales, head of product, etc.)
- A clear path to Series B milestones (usually $5–10M ARR with strong growth)

### Series B ($15M–$50M+)

The bet is on **scaling to a category-defining outcome**. Investors want:

- $5M+ ARR, ideally growing >100% YoY
- Strong cohort metrics: net retention, expansion, payback
- Clear unit economics with confidence in CAC and LTV
- Multi-channel acquisition working
- Senior leadership team mostly in seat
- A defensible position vs. clear competitors
- A path to $100M ARR within 24–36 months

## How investors decide to reply

Three things move them from "interesting" to "I'll take the call":

1. **Specificity.** Real numbers, real customers, real founder background. The opposite of generic.
2. **Thesis fit.** The founder did the work to understand what this partner cares about, and the email actually fits that thesis. (This is why the personalization line matters more than founders think.)
3. **Momentum.** "Closing soon" is fake unless it's true. Real momentum is "$1.2M committed, 6 calls this week, targeting close by [date]." Investors are pattern-matchers and they want to be in deals that other people they respect want to be in.

## How investors decide to pass

The fastest passes happen on:

- Bad market fit (not what they invest in)
- Bad stage fit (too early or too late)
- Competing portfolio company (auto-pass)
- Geographic mismatch (especially for funds that only do SF/NYC)
- Founder pattern-mismatch (they have a strong implicit model of "who wins in this space")
- Anti-signals from the email itself (see above)

The slower passes happen during DD on:

- Customer references that don't validate the founder's claims
- Numbers that fall apart on detailed inspection
- Cap table issues (over-dilution, weird preferences, unclear ownership)
- Founder dynamics issues (uneven equity, unclear roles, churn)
- Tech debt or single-point-of-failure issues
- Unit economics that don't have a credible path to working

## Writing for the partner, not the firm

A common founder mistake: writing to "the firm" rather than the partner.

The firm's website talks about "we invest in founders building..." That's marketing. The actual partner has their own thesis, their own active themes, their own portfolio companies, and their own track record. The partner is the one who'll champion you to the partnership meeting.

Find the right partner. Read their writing. Check their recent investments. Reference something specific. The personalization line is doing more work than founders realize: it's signaling "I researched you specifically, I'm not mass-blasting."

## Pattern recognition: what the partner is matching against

Investors think in patterns. When they read your email, they're trying to map you to:

- Founders they've backed before who succeeded
- Companies they've passed on that they regret passing on
- Adjacent companies in their portfolio
- Categories they're actively trying to add to their portfolio

You can help the partner pattern-match by being explicit about what kind of company you are. "We're the [Stripe / Notion / Datadog / Airbnb] of [your domain]" is a cliché but it works because it gives the partner an instant frame. Just make sure the analogy holds up.

Better: be specific about what kind of trajectory you're on. "We're following a similar pattern to [comp at the same stage]: same wedge into [segment], same expansion path, our retention curve looks similar at month 6." That's a much harder claim to make and lands much harder when true.

## The momentum game

The single biggest force in fundraising is momentum. Investors want to be in deals that are moving. Investors don't want to be the first dollar in unless they have very high conviction.

How to build momentum:

1. Build a list of 30–50 target investors before you start, ranked by tier.
2. Start with tier 3 to practice your pitch. Get to "no" or "yes" fast on these.
3. Once your pitch is sharp, hit tier 2 in the same 1–2 week window.
4. With a few "yes" or "leaning yes" in hand, hit tier 1 with the momentum signal. "I have $X soft-circled, you'd be one of the first leads to look at it. I'm closing in [N] weeks."

Avoid: blasting tier 1 first, getting a string of passes, and then trying to come back later with the same pitch. That signal is bad.

## Why founders should care about all this

Because the bar is brutally high and the founder's energy is finite. Sending 100 generic emails to whoever you can find on Crunchbase will get you 1–2 replies on a good week. Sending 20 carefully-researched emails to thesis-fit partners with strong personalization can get you 8–12 replies, several first meetings, and one or two paths to a check.

That's the difference this skill is trying to deliver: 5–10x conversion lift on the same fundraise effort. Quality compounds in fundraising more than quantity.
