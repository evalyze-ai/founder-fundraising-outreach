# Style Guide: Founder Voice for Investor Outreach

The voice rules that separate founder writing from press-release VC-speak. Internalize these. Apply to every output.

## The voice in one paragraph

Sound like a real founder talking to a smart peer, not a press release. Confident, not arrogant. Direct, not blunt. Warm, not gushy. Short sentences, but not slogans. Active voice. Concrete nouns. Simple words. One number that matters, not four. The investor should finish reading and think "this person knows what they're doing," not "this is well-written marketing copy."

## Banned words and phrases (with replacements)

These trigger pattern-match alarms in investors. Cut them.

| Banned | Why | Use instead |
|---|---|---|
| synergy | corporate, vague | name the actual thing that fits together |
| paradigm (shift) | empty | describe what changed |
| leverage (as a verb) | corporate | use "use" |
| unlock | overused buzzword | "enable," or describe the thing |
| supercharge / turbocharge | AI hype verb | give the metric impact |
| revolutionize | huge red flag | describe the specific change |
| disrupt / disruptive | exhausted | describe what you're displacing |
| game-changer | empty | what specifically changes |
| best-in-class / world-class | no proof | give the proof |
| next-gen / cutting-edge | empty | name the actual technology |
| mission-critical | corporate | "core to their workflow" or specific |
| seamless | overused | describe what's smooth and how |
| robust solution / platform | corporate-speak | "tool" or specific noun |
| end-to-end | vague | describe the actual span |
| democratize | overused buzzword | "make accessible to" + specific group |
| AI-powered | meaningless without specifics | name the actual model/method/result |
| empower / empowering | corporate | name the action it enables |
| transform / transformation | empty | describe the before and after |
| reimagine / reinvent | empty | describe the change |
| holistic | vague | describe the parts |
| ecosystem | overused | name the parts |
| value proposition | corporate | describe the value |
| stakeholder | corporate | name them |
| at scale | vague | give the scale |
| level up / 10x | meme | give the actual multiple |
| impact (bare) / insights / alignment / momentum / efficiency / scalability | 2026 LinkedIn-average polish | say the concrete thing |
| genuine / meaningful / truly / real impact | hollow intensifiers | cut, or prove it with a fact |
| utilize / facilitate / procure | dressed-up verbs | use / help / buy |

Simple wording is a rule, not a preference. If a smart 12 year old would stumble on a word, swap it. AI reaches for the fancy synonym; a busy human reaches for the short one.

## Banned opening lines

These get deleted before sentence two.

- "Sorry to bother you, but..."
- "I know you're incredibly busy..."
- "Hope this finds you well..."
- "I'm reaching out because..."
- "I came across your profile..."
- "I'm a long-time fan of your work..."
- "I admire your investment thesis..."
- "I've been following [Firm] for years..."
- "Quick question..." (when it's not quick)
- "I'll be brief..." (and then aren't)
- "Since you're [doing X]..." (presuming their situation without a citable source)

**The opener that works when it's true:** the founder's real identity in plain words. "I'm a practicing cardiologist and the founder of X." "I spent six years buying this software before I built it." When the founder's day job IS the insight, that line beats any researched hook, and it can't be faked by a tool because it's a checkable fact about a person.

## Banned closing lines

- "Let me know if you're interested." (Weak. Replace with a specific ask.)
- "Looking forward to hearing from you." (Filler.)
- "Happy to chat anytime." (Specific or it's empty.)
- "Quick coffee chat?" (Without context, this signals you have nothing specific to say.)
- "Would love to connect." (Why? About what?)
- "Open to feedback." (Don't ask for feedback in a cold email. Ask for a reply.)

**The ask that works:** one line, alone, under 12 words, ends with a question mark, numerals for minutes. "Worth 20 minutes next week?" "Want the deck?" "Open to a quick call Tuesday?" Field data: these short question asks got replies; the two-sentence throat-clearing asks did not.

## Links, attachments, and the first email

**Nothing clickable in a first touch.** No deck link, no Calendly, no website link, no PDF. Three reasons, all evidenced:

1. Deliverability: links and attachments are the heaviest spam-filter weights in cold sends (Mailpool, Woodpecker, Overloop, 2025-2026 guidance all converge here).
2. Engagement: asking permission to send the deck outperforms dropping it. The "want the deck?" reply is a micro-commitment that starts a thread.
3. Read: an email with nothing to click reads like a person, not a campaign.

The deck goes out after they reply, as a trackable link (DocSend or similar), ideally within the hour. Have it ready before the campaign starts.

## The punctuation diet

Punctuation is now an AI-detection surface. Full evidence in `anti-ai-tells.md`. The rules:

**Allowed:** periods, commas, question marks, parentheses sparingly, straight quotes only. Hyphens in ranges ($250-500K, Tue-Thu) and fixed tokens (B2B, 510(k)).

**Banned in all output:**
- **Em dashes.** The loudest single AI tell. Investors notice.
- **Semicolons.** Now AI-associated too, and they were always overdressed for a cold email anyway.
- **En dashes as sentence breaks.** Caught in the same net. Ranges are fine.
- **Colons in cold emails,** body or subject. The colon-label cadence ("The challenge: ... The solution: ...") is pure AI list-machinery, and even a single colon in a 60-word email reads like formatting, not typing.
- **Stacked exclamation points.** One max per email, warm contexts only, never in a cold first touch.
- **Curly quotes and apostrophes.** Chatbot copy-paste artifact. Straight quotes only.
- **Emoji.** None. The rocket is a documented AI fingerprint.

**Drop optional compound hyphens in email prose.** "Early stage fund," not "early-stage fund." "First time founder," not "first-time founder." Perfect compound hyphenation is edited-prose register; people typing fast skip it. Keep a hyphen only where dropping it creates a real misread. Combined with the dash bans, the target is an email with zero horizontal lines in it anywhere.

**Underpunctuate on purpose.** Roughly one comma per sentence or fewer. Don't button every clause. An occasional comma splice is human and welcome:

**Overdressed:** "We launched in March; since then, revenue has doubled."
**Human:** "We launched in March, revenue doubled since."

**Bad:** "We're building Quill - the operations layer for hospitals - and we just hit $50K MRR."
**Good (sentence break):** "We're building Quill, the operations layer for hospitals. Just hit $50K MRR."

Punctuation formality also scales down as the channel gets more casual (the Binghamton period studies: formally perfect punctuation in casual messages reads stiff and insincere). But never costume it: no all-lowercase gimmick, no fake typos. Forced casualness over generic content is now its own documented tell (see `anti-ai-tells.md` Layer 7). The specifics carry the human signal; the light punctuation just stays out of their way.

## Burstiness: vary the rhythm on purpose

AI text has uniform sentence lengths and rectangle paragraphs. Readers feel it; detectors score it. 2026 detection guidance weights metronomic rhythm above every vocabulary signal. Break it deliberately:

- At least one sentence under 7 words and one over 18 in every email.
- Never three consecutive sentences within about 3 words of the same length.
- Paragraphs visibly uneven. A one-line paragraph is good.
- Starting with And, But, or So is allowed and occasionally encouraged.
- Maximum ONE sentence fragment per email. Never stacked ("Short. Isolated. Reflective." is a known tell), never the "No X. No Y. Just Z." shape, never the negation-contrast fragment ("It's pre-seed. But not like other pre-seed."). When every line lands like a slogan, the email reads like a copy deck.
- No bullets in cold emails under 80 words. In longer documents, at most one bulleted moment, 2-3 items of uneven length, no bold labels.

**Flat (AI rhythm):** "We help hospitals manage operations. Our platform replaces six legacy tools. Teams save ten hours every week. The integration takes about two days."

**Fake-punchy (also AI):** "Six tools. One workspace. Ten hours back every week."

**Human rhythm:** "We help hospitals run their operations out of one workspace instead of six legacy tools, which is the part buyers don't believe until they see it. Teams get about 10 hours a week back."

## The four-block shape

Every cold email is four blocks with a blank line between each. On a phone this renders as four light touches instead of a wall, and it is the shape that got replies in real 2025-2026 investor campaigns.

1. **Greeting.** "Hi [First],"
2. **Body.** Who I am, what this is, the one proof point. One to two short paragraphs.
3. **The ask.** Alone on its own line. Under 12 words, ends with a question mark.
4. **Sign-off.** First name, or "Best," then first name on the next line.

Keep the shape. Rotate everything inside it.

## One proof point, not a metric wall

The old advice was "pack the traction line with numbers." That now reads as machine compression, and it splits the reader's attention four ways.

| Metric wall (retired) | One proof point (current) |
|---|---|
| $40K MRR, 35% MoM, 11 hospitals, 92% retention | 11 hospitals pay for it and 92% have stayed |
| 50K waitlist, 80% organic, beta in Nov, 2 design partners | 50K joined the waitlist in six weeks, all organic |
| 3 patents, 6 LOIs, $150/seat/month, 4,000 target sites | about 4,000 sites in the US still do this by hand, we have signed LOIs with the first of them |

One fact they remember beats four they skim. The other proof points are ammunition for follow-ups (each follow-up leads with a different one) and the deck.

## Numbers, not adjectives

| Adjective version | Number version |
|---|---|
| Strong growth | revenue doubled since March |
| Lots of customer interest | 14 paying customers |
| World-class team | both founders ex-Stripe, shipped payments to 200M users |
| Great retention | 92% of customers stayed past a year |
| Rapid adoption | 50K signups in 6 weeks |
| Game-changing | cuts admin from 3 hours to 20 minutes per shift |

Pick ONE per email. Accuracy rule: never state a number or status one notch stronger than the founder's own wording, especially regulatory and legal claims. "Submission planned for Q3" and "cleared" are different universes, "patent pending" and "patented" too, and investors check.

## Email length rules

2026 benchmark data (Instantly, Woodpecker 20M-email dataset) puts peak reply rates under 80 words for first touch. Real campaigns run under these rules booked meetings at 45-75 words.

| Email type | Length |
|---|---|
| Cold email body | 40-70 words ideal, 80 hard cap |
| Subject line | Under 55 characters |
| Follow-up 1 | 30-55 words |
| Follow-up 2 | 30-55 words |
| Follow-up 3 | 40-70 words |
| Reply (direct version) | 60-120 words |
| Reply (warmer version) | 100-180 words |
| Post-meeting recap | 50-120 words |

If the founder's draft is over these, cut. Most founders' first drafts are 2x too long. Shorter is not just safer, it converts better and it reads more human, because nobody busy writes 150 words to a stranger.

## Paragraph rules

- No paragraph longer than 4 lines.
- Single-sentence paragraphs are fine and often better.
- Blank line between every structural block (greeting, body, ask, sign-off).
- White space is your friend, especially on mobile, where most VCs read first.

## Personalization that works

- ONE specific reference to something the partner wrote, said, or invested in. One line. Then move on.
- A reference to a portfolio company that's adjacent (not competitor), framed as thesis fit: "you led [Company]'s seed, same buyer, different wedge."
- A mutual connection (only with permission to use their name).
- The founder's own checkable identity, which is personalization in reverse and often stronger than anything about the recipient.

Fit beats flattery. The thesis-match line does more work than personal details.

## Personalization that fails

- "I've followed [Firm] for years" / "I admire your portfolio" / "big fan of your work"
- "Your fund's thesis aligns with our vision"
- Two or more researched personal details in one email (reads as enrichment-tool output)
- Name-dropping the General Partner when you're emailing a different partner
- "Saw you went to [school]" (creepy unless you went there too and have a real reason)
- Quoting their podcast back at them at length (one phrase, once, is the ceiling)

## Subject line patterns that work

Under 55 characters, sentence case, no colons, no dashes. Commas are the only separator. It should read like something a person typed, not a template with slots.

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| sector + one number | "b2b payments, $1.2M ARR" |
| problem + who | "medication tracking, the sites nobody builds for" |
| stage + signal | "seed round, 3x yoy growth" |
| portfolio adjacency | "[portfolio company] overlap" |
| mutual connection | "[Mutual] said to reach out" |
| investor signal | "re your vertical AI post" |

## Subject line patterns that fail

- "Investment opportunity" / "Funding round"
- "Quick chat?" / "Introduction" / "Hi from [Founder]"
- "[Company] - Pitch Deck" or anything with a dash or colon in it
- Title Case Every Word (reads as marketing automation)
- "Following up" on a first email (nothing to follow up to)
- Anything over 55 characters (gets truncated on phones anyway)

## Active voice always

| Passive (bad) | Active (good) |
|---|---|
| The product was launched in March. | We launched in March. |
| Revenue has grown to $50K. | We grew revenue to $50K. |
| Customers are being onboarded weekly. | We onboard 4 customers a week. |

## Concrete nouns over abstractions

| Abstract (bad) | Concrete (good) |
|---|---|
| Solutions | Tool / app / API / dashboard / device |
| Platform | Whatever the actual product is |
| Stakeholders | Customers / users / clinics / partners |
| Vertical | Hospitals / accounting firms / construction crews |
| Resources | People / dollars / time |
| Capabilities | The actual feature |

## Register matching

This is one of the most underrated rules. Match the investor's register.

- They wrote 2 lines? You write 2 lines.
- They wrote a thoughtful paragraph? Match the warmth, but stay tight.
- They wrote casually with lower-case "i"? You can be slightly more casual.
- They wrote a formal "Dear [name]"? Bring it up a notch.

Mismatched register feels off and breaks rapport.

## Before / After example

### Before

> Subject: Investment opportunity in revolutionary AI platform
>
> Dear Mr. Patel,
>
> I hope this email finds you well. My name is Mike Chen, and I am the CEO and Co-Founder of Quill. I have been a long-time admirer of Greylock and your incredible portfolio of disruptive companies.
>
> Quill is a revolutionary, AI-powered, end-to-end operations platform that is reimagining the way mid-market hospitals operate. We are leveraging cutting-edge AI to unlock significant value for our customers and democratize access to enterprise-grade tooling.
>
> We have been seeing strong growth and have signed up multiple customers across the country. We believe we are positioned to be a game-changer in this $50B+ market.
>
> We are currently raising our seed round and would love to leverage your expertise. Would you be open to a quick chat sometime to discuss this exciting opportunity? I have attached our pitch deck for your review.
>
> Looking forward to hearing back from you.
>
> Best regards,
> Michael Chen
> CEO and Co-Founder, Quill Inc.

**184 words, attachment, six blacklist words, zero facts.**

### After

> Subject: hospital ops software, 11 hospitals paying
>
> Hi Sarah,
>
> I ran operations at a 300 bed hospital for six years before starting Quill. We replace the six tools hospitals juggle for ops with one workspace. 11 hospitals pay for it now and 92% have stayed.
>
> Your vertical AI post last week matched what we see in the field, so you were the first call on my list.
>
> Raising our seed. Worth 20 minutes next week?
>
> Mike

**68 words. One proof point (11 paying, 92% stayed). One recipient detail, one line. No links, no attachment, no deck. Identity opener that only this founder could write. Four blocks, blank lines between. Zero dashes anywhere, including "300 bed" and "vertical AI post." The deck goes out when Sarah replies.**

### What changed and why

- **Subject:** template with a colon and hype → six plain words with one number, under 55 chars, comma as the only separator.
- **Opener:** flattery → the founder's checkable identity. Nobody's AI tool writes "I ran operations at a 300 bed hospital for six years" unless it's true, and Sarah knows that.
- **Metrics:** the old version had zero facts; the tempting overcorrection is four facts in one line, which reads machine-compressed. One proof point carries it.
- **Ask:** "quick chat sometime" → seven words ending in a question mark.
- **Length:** 184 → 68. The 2026 reply-rate data and the human read both want this.
- **Clickables:** attachment → nothing. Sarah's reply is the deliverable; the deck rides on it.

## When to break the rules

These rules are guidelines, not commandments. Break them when:

- The investor's register is unusually formal (you can match)
- You have a personal relationship that warrants warmth (you can be longer)
- You're replying to someone who asked for detail (don't artificially shorten)
- The founder's verified voice sample genuinely conflicts on a low-risk item (their style wins, except on high-risk tells)

The point is to sound like a real, smart, busy founder. Not to follow rules robotically.
