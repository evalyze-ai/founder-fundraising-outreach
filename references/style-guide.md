# Style Guide: Founder Voice for Investor Outreach

The voice rules that separate founder writing from press-release VC-speak. Internalize these. Apply to every output.

## The voice in one paragraph

Sound like a real founder talking to a smart peer, not a press release. Confident, not arrogant. Direct, not blunt. Warm, not gushy. Short sentences. Active voice. Concrete nouns. Numbers, not adjectives. The investor should finish reading and think "this person knows what they're doing," not "this is well-written marketing copy."

## Banned words and phrases (with replacements)

These trigger pattern-match alarms in investors. Cut them.

| Banned | Why | Use instead |
|---|---|---|
| synergy | corporate, vague | name the actual thing that fits together |
| paradigm (shift) | empty | describe what changed |
| leverage (as a verb) | corporate | use "use" |
| unlock | overused buzzword | "enable," or describe the thing |
| supercharge | meaningless | give the metric impact |
| revolutionize | huge red flag | describe the specific change |
| disrupt / disruptive | exhausted | describe what you're displacing |
| game-changer | empty | what specifically changes |
| best-in-class | no proof | give the proof |
| world-class | no proof | give the proof |
| next-gen | empty | describe what's different |
| cutting-edge | empty | name the actual technology |
| mission-critical | corporate | "core to their workflow" or specific |
| seamless | overused | describe what's smooth and how |
| robust solution / platform | corporate-speak | "tool" or specific noun |
| end-to-end | vague | describe the actual span |
| democratize | overused buzzword | "make accessible to" + specific group |
| AI-powered | meaningless without specifics | name the actual model/method/result |
| empower / empowering | corporate | name the action it enables |
| transform / transformation | empty | describe the before and after |
| reimagine / reinvent | empty | describe the change |
| holistic | vague | describe the parts |
| ecosystem | overused | name the parts |
| value proposition | corporate | describe the value |
| stakeholder | corporate | name them |
| at scale | vague | give the scale |
| level up | meme | describe the upgrade |
| 10x | overused | give the actual multiple |

## Banned opening lines

These get deleted before sentence two.

- "Sorry to bother you, but..."
- "I know you're incredibly busy..."
- "Hope this finds you well..."
- "I'm reaching out because..."
- "I came across your profile..."
- "I'm a long-time fan of your work..."
- "I admire your investment thesis..."
- "I've been following [Firm] for years..."
- "Quick question..." (when it's not quick)
- "I'll be brief..." (and then aren't)

## Banned closing lines

- "Let me know if you're interested." (Weak. Replace with a specific ask.)
- "Looking forward to hearing from you." (Filler.)
- "Happy to chat anytime." (Specific time or it's empty.)
- "Quick coffee chat?" (Without context, this signals you have nothing specific to say.)
- "Would love to connect." (Why? About what?)
- "Open to feedback." (Don't ask for feedback in a cold email. Ask for a meeting.)

## Em dashes are banned

Replace em dashes with commas, parentheses, or sentence breaks. This isn't aesthetic preference, em dashes are one of the strongest tells of LLM-generated text in 2026, and investors notice.

**Bad:** "We're building Quill - the operations layer for hospitals - and we just hit $50K MRR."

**Good (commas):** "We're building Quill, the operations layer for hospitals, and we just hit $50K MRR."

**Good (parentheses):** "We're building Quill (the operations layer for hospitals), and we just hit $50K MRR."

**Good (sentence break):** "We're building Quill, the operations layer for hospitals. Just hit $50K MRR."

## Numbers, not adjectives

| Adjective version | Number version |
|---|---|
| Strong growth | $40K MRR, growing 35% MoM for 6 months |
| Lots of customer interest | 14 paying customers, 22 signed pilots |
| World-class team | Two founders, both ex-Stripe, shipped payments to 200M users |
| Massive market | $X bottom-up TAM in our ICP, derived from [N] companies × [$Y] ACP |
| Great retention | 92% logo retention at 12 months, 118% NRR |
| Best-in-class product | NPS of 67, 4.8 average rating from 200+ reviews |
| Rapid adoption | 50K signups in 6 weeks, 80% organic |
| Significant traction | $200K monthly GMV, 40% take rate |
| Game-changing | Cuts hospital admin time from 3 hours to 20 minutes per shift |

## Email length rules

| Email type | Length |
|---|---|
| Cold email body | 100–130 words ideal, 150 absolute max |
| Subject line | Under 60 characters |
| Follow-up 1 | 50–80 words |
| Follow-up 2 | 100–150 words |
| Follow-up 3 | 60–100 words |
| Reply (direct version) | 60–120 words |
| Reply (warmer version) | 100–180 words |
| Thank-you after meeting | 100–150 words |

If the founder's draft is over these, cut. Most founders' first drafts are 2x too long.

## Paragraph rules

- No paragraph longer than 4 lines.
- Single-sentence paragraphs are fine and often better.
- White space is your friend, especially on mobile, where most VCs read first.

## Personalization that works

- A specific reference to something the partner wrote, said, or invested in
- A reference to a portfolio company that's adjacent (not competitor)
- A specific market insight you both share
- A mutual connection (only with permission to use their name)

## Personalization that fails

- "I've followed [Firm] for years"
- "I admire your portfolio"
- "Your fund's thesis aligns with our vision"
- "I'm a big fan of your work"
- Name-dropping the General Partner when you're emailing a different partner
- "Saw you went to [school]" (creepy unless you went there too and have a real reason)

## Subject line patterns that work

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| Company + value prop + traction | "Quill: AI ops for mid-market hospitals - $40K MRR" |
| Sector + stage + traction | "Vertical AI for accountants, pre-seed, 14 paying customers" |
| Problem + traction signal | "Cutting hospital admin from 3hrs to 20min, 11 systems on board" |
| Mutual connection | "[Mutual] suggested I reach out - [Company], [traction]" |
| Reference to investor signal | "Re your post on vertical AI moats - [Company]" |

## Subject line patterns that fail

- "Investment opportunity"
- "Quick chat?"
- "Hi from [Founder]"
- "Introduction"
- "[Company] - Pitch Deck"
- "Following up" (on a first email; nothing to follow up to)
- "Funding round" (vague and dull)

## Active voice always

| Passive (bad) | Active (good) |
|---|---|
| The product was launched in March. | We launched the product in March. |
| Revenue has grown to $50K. | We grew revenue to $50K. |
| Customers are being onboarded weekly. | We onboard 4 customers a week. |

## Concrete nouns over abstractions

| Abstract (bad) | Concrete (good) |
|---|---|
| Solutions | Tool / app / API / dashboard / model |
| Platform | Whatever the actual product is |
| Stakeholders | Customers / users / employees / partners |
| Vertical | Hospitals / accounting firms / construction crews |
| Resources | People / dollars / time |
| Capabilities | The actual feature |

## Register matching

This is one of the most underrated rules. Match the investor's register.

- They wrote 2 lines? You write 2 lines.
- They wrote a thoughtful paragraph? Match the warmth, but stay tight.
- They wrote casually with lower-case "i"? You can be slightly more casual.
- They wrote a formal "Dear [name]"? Bring it up a notch.

Mismatched register feels off and breaks rapport.

## Before / After examples

### Cold email - before

> Subject: Investment opportunity in revolutionary AI platform
>
> Dear Mr. Patel,
>
> I hope this email finds you well. My name is Mike Chen, and I am the CEO and Co-Founder of Quill. I have been a long-time admirer of Greylock and your incredible portfolio of disruptive companies.
>
> Quill is a revolutionary, AI-powered, end-to-end operations platform that is reimagining the way mid-market hospitals operate. We are leveraging cutting-edge AI to unlock significant value for our customers and democratize access to enterprise-grade tooling.
>
> We have been seeing strong growth and have signed up multiple customers across the country. We believe we are positioned to be a game-changer in this $50B+ market.
>
> We are currently raising our seed round and would love to leverage your expertise. Would you be open to a quick chat sometime to discuss this exciting opportunity?
>
> Looking forward to hearing back from you.
>
> Best regards,
> Michael Chen
> CEO and Co-Founder, Quill Inc.

### Cold email - after

> Subject: AI ops for mid-market hospitals - $40K MRR, 35% MoM
>
> Hi Sarah,
>
> Caught your post on vertical AI in regulated industries last week. The point about workflow lock-in mattering more than model quality matched what we're seeing.
>
> Building Quill, the operations layer for mid-market hospitals (50–500 beds). Replaces 6 legacy tools with one workspace, integrates with Epic and Cerner.
>
> $40K MRR, growing 35% MoM, 11 paying hospitals, 92% retention. Two regional health systems in pilot, one signs in November.
>
> Raising $3M seed. $1.2M soft-circled from healthcare angels and Mercury Health. Looking for a thesis-fit lead.
>
> 25 minutes next week? Tue/Wed afternoons PST. Calendly: [link]. Deck: [link]
>
> Mike

### What changed and why

- Subject line: "Investment opportunity" said nothing. New version says exactly what the company is and gives Sarah a number to pattern-match against.
- Opening: "Hope this email finds you well" was filler. Removed. Replaced with a real, specific reference to something Sarah wrote, with one sentence proving the founder read it.
- Buzzword density: "revolutionary," "AI-powered," "end-to-end," "reimagining," "leveraging cutting-edge," "unlock significant value," "democratize," "strong growth," "game-changer." Every one cut. Replaced with concrete nouns and numbers.
- Length: 184 words → 110 words.
- Ask: "quick chat sometime" → "25 minutes next week, Tue/Wed afternoons PST, here's my Calendly."
- Sign-off: "Best regards, Michael Chen, CEO and Co-Founder, Quill Inc." → "Mike."

## When to break the rules

These rules are guidelines, not commandments. Break them when:

- The investor's register is unusually formal (you can match)
- You have a personal relationship that warrants warmth (you can be longer)
- You're following up on a meeting (the rules shift, see follow-up patterns)
- The investor explicitly asked for more detail (don't artificially shorten)

The point is to sound like a real, smart, busy founder. Not to follow rules robotically.
