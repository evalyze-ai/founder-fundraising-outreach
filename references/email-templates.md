# Email Templates and Patterns

Detailed templates and patterns for every cold email, follow-up, and reply scenario. These are not fill-in-the-blank templates. They are skeletons to adapt. Always rewrite in the founder's actual voice.

**Mandatory gate (v6):** every skeleton in this file passes through the Human-voice system in SKILL.md and the scrub in `anti-ai-tells.md` before it ships. That means: no blacklist words, none of the thirteen banned constructions, the punctuation diet (including dropped optional hyphens and no colons in cold emails), the burstiness rules, and structure rotation. Plus the v6 hard rules for first-touch cold emails: **40-70 words (80 cap), zero links or attachments or calendar links, exactly one proof point, at most one recipient detail, the four-block shape with blank lines between blocks, subject under 55 chars with no colons or dashes, ask under 12 words ending in a question mark, numerals for minutes.** Never reuse one skeleton shape across multiple drafts in a session; if two founders compared the emails this skill wrote them, the skeletons should not look like siblings. Where any older skeleton below conflicts with those rules (a labeled traction block, a stacked metric string, a deck link in a first touch, matching bullets, a triple-phrase cadence), the v6 rules win. The worked examples below have been updated to the v6 shape; where a longer legacy example survives for a reply scenario, treat its strategy as current and its length as the ceiling, not the target.

## 1. Cold email patterns

### Pattern A: Hook from a recent signal

When the investor wrote something, said something, or invested in something relevant in the last 30-60 days. This is the highest-conversion hook because it shows you actually pay attention.

**Skeleton (four blocks, blank line between each):**

> Subject: [sector or problem, one number, commas only, under 55 chars]
>
> Hi [First],
>
> Your [post / podcast / talk] on [specific topic] matched what we see at [Company]. We're building [what it is, plain words] for [who]. [ONE proof point, the strongest single fact.]
>
> Raising [$X] [stage]. Worth 20 minutes next week?
>
> [First name]

No deck link, no calendar link, nothing clickable. The deck goes out when they reply.

**Worked example (v6 shape; the pattern that booked meetings in real 2025-2026 campaigns):**

> Subject: hospital ops software, 11 hospitals paying
>
> Hi Sarah,
>
> Your post on vertical AI in regulated industries matched what we see in the field. We're building Quill, one workspace that replaces the six tools mid-market hospitals juggle for operations. 11 hospitals pay for it now and 92% have stayed.
>
> Raising our seed. Worth 20 minutes next week?
>
> Mike

**Why this works:**
- 52 words. Under the 2026 reply-rate sweet spot and readable in one phone glance.
- Subject is six plain words with one number, no colon, no dash, could only be this company.
- One recipient detail (her post), one line, then move on.
- One proof point (11 paying, 92% stayed), not a metric wall. MRR and growth rate are held back for follow-up 1 and the deck.
- Ask is seven words ending in a question mark, numerals for the minutes.
- Nothing to click, so nothing for a spam filter to score and nothing that reads like a campaign. Her reply is the goal, the deck rides on it.

### Pattern B: Hook from a portfolio adjacency

When the investor backed a company you're adjacent to (not a competitor, but in a related space). Use this when there's no recent signal but a clear thesis-fit.

**Skeleton:**

> Subject: [portfolio company] overlap, [Company]
>
> [Investor first name],
>
> Saw [Firm] led [Portfolio Co]'s round. We're solving an adjacent problem: [your one-liner].
>
> [Two-line on what it is, who for, what's different.]
>
> [Traction line.]
>
> [Round status.]
>
> [Ask + deck.]

**Don't use this** if the portfolio company is a direct competitor. Skip that investor.

### Pattern C: Mutual connection (warm-cold)

When you have a mutual connection but haven't gotten a formal warm intro. Better than full cold, weaker than a real intro. Useful when the connection is busy and you don't want to wait.

**Skeleton:**

> Subject: [Mutual] said to reach out, [Company]
>
> [Investor first name],
>
> [Mutual] mentioned you'd be a good person to talk to about [Company]. [Mutual] knows us through [context, one line].
>
> [Two-line on what it is and traction.]
>
> [Round status. Ask. Deck.]

**Important:** ask the mutual connection first if they're OK with you using their name. If they offer a real intro, take that instead and skip this pattern.

### Pattern D: Pre-revenue / very early stage

When traction is thin (waitlist, no revenue, just-launched). Lean on team and insight, not numbers.

**Skeleton (pre-revenue: the founder's identity IS the proof point):**

> Subject: [problem or wedge, plain words, under 55 chars]
>
> Hi [First],
>
> [Identity line: why this founder, checkable and plain.] Building [what it is, one sentence]. [ONE early signal: waitlist, LOIs, design partners. Honest about scale.]
>
> Raising [$X] pre-seed. Open to 20 minutes?
>
> [First name]

**Worked example (consumer AI, pre-revenue, v6 shape):**

> Subject: voice journaling, 50K waitlist in six weeks
>
> Hi James,
>
> I spent three years at Snap shipping voice features, now I'm building Mira, voice first journaling. You talk, it gives you back a structured reflection. 50K people joined the waitlist in six weeks, all organic.
>
> Your essay on consumer AI being a UX problem is half our thesis.
>
> Raising $1M pre-seed. Open to 20 minutes?
>
> Priya

**Why this works:**
- 58 words. The team line is the opener because pre-revenue, the founder is the product.
- One proof point (the waitlist), one recipient detail (his essay, one line).
- "voice first" with no hyphen, "50K" plain, no colons, nothing clickable.
- Angel commits and beta date held back for follow-up 1, which now has something new to say.

## 2. Follow-up sequence patterns

### Follow-up 1 - 3 business days after the cold email

30-55 words. Reply in the same thread, no new subject line. Never open with "following up," "circling back," "bumping this," or "just checking in." Lead with the new fact itself, or "One thing I left out last time:", "Quick update:", or a different proof point than the first email carried.

**Skeleton:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> One thing I left out last time: [new fact, or the second-strongest proof point held back from email 1].
>
> [Restate the ask, one line, question mark.]
>
> [First name]

**Example:**

> Hi Sarah,
>
> One thing I left out last time: we signed our 12th hospital this morning, the second biggest in our pipeline.
>
> Still worth 20 minutes next week?
>
> Mike

**If there's nothing new to share:** be plain and offer the easy no.

> Hi Sarah,
>
> I know your inbox is brutal. If this isn't a fit for you right now, a quick no is genuinely useful and I'll stop here.
>
> Mike

### Follow-up 2 - 7-10 days after Follow-up 1

Different angle. Share an insight, a customer learning, a content piece you wrote, or a forward-looking metric. Make it interesting on its own merit, even if they don't reply.

**Skeleton:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Quick update: [one insight from a customer call, one market data point, or one metric move. Two lines max.]
>
> [Restate the ask, one line, question mark. Or offer the deck: "Want the deck?"]
>
> [First name]

**Example:**

> Hi Sarah,
>
> Quick update: spent yesterday with three CIOs at 200 bed hospitals, every one is being told to do AI by their board with no budget and no plan. That gap is exactly our wedge. We also crossed $50K MRR this week.
>
> Worth 20 minutes, Tue or Thu?
>
> Mike

**Why this works:**
- Leads with the new thing, never with "following up."
- The customer-call insight shows the founder is in the field, and the metric shows momentum since email 1 (the MRR number was deliberately held out of the first touch).
- 48 words. Still no link; the deck is one reply away, which is the point.

### Follow-up 3 - 2-3 weeks after Follow-up 2

Final and graceful. Restate the ask, make it easy to say no, leave the door open.

**Skeleton:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Going to stop chasing after this one. If timing's not right or it's not a fit for [Firm] right now, I get it.
>
> If you're up for it, mind if I send an update in 60-90 days? I'll have [specific milestone] by then.
>
> Either way, thanks for the time you've already spent.
>
> [First name]

**Example:**

> Hi Sarah,
>
> Going to stop chasing on this. If timing's not right or it's not a fit for Bessemer right now, totally understand.
>
> Mind if I send an update in 60 days? Should be at $80-100K MRR by then with our first enterprise contract closed.
>
> Either way, appreciate the time you've already given the inbox.
>
> Mike

**Why this works:**
- Confident exit. No begging.
- Specific milestone shows where the company will be, gives the investor a reason to want the next update.
- Leaves door open without being needy.

### Optional Follow-up 4 - Re-engagement after a polite pass

Send 60-90 days after a "not now." Only if there's something genuinely new.

**Skeleton:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Said I'd send an update. Quick one:
>
> - [Metric movement: revenue, customers, retention]
> - [Big milestone: new hire, partnership, customer logo]
> - [Round status: closed, closing, expanded]
>
> No ask, just promised I'd close the loop.
>
> [If next round is approaching:] Will likely be back in the market for [Series X] in [timing].
>
> [First name]

**Example:**

> Hi Sarah,
>
> Said I'd send an update. Quick one:
>
> - $90K MRR (was $40K when we last talked), 32% MoM still
> - Closed first 500-bed system in October, two more in pilot
> - Hired Anjali as VP Eng (ex-Verily, ran their hospital product)
> - Closed our seed at $4M, Mercury Health led
>
> No ask. Said I'd close the loop, so closing it.
>
> Will likely be back in the market for our Series A in Q3 next year. Hope to talk then.
>
> Mike

## 3. Reply patterns by scenario

For every reply, produce TWO versions (direct + warmer) so the founder picks based on the relationship.

### Scenario: "Send me your deck"

**Direct version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Here you go: [trackable deck link]
>
> Quick teaser: [Company] is [one-liner], doing [traction line]. Slides 4 and 9 are the main story.
>
> Open to a 25 min call next Tue or Wed if useful?
>
> [First name]

**Warmer version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Thanks for taking a look. Here's the deck: [trackable link]
>
> Quick context before you click through: we're [one-liner]. The thing I'd flag is slide 9, where the cohort retention curve sits. That's the chart that surprised me most when we ran it.
>
> Currently at [traction line] with [round status one-liner].
>
> Happy to walk through it on a call if you want, 25 min any day next week other than Friday. Otherwise, all good if you'd rather review and come back with questions.
>
> [First name]

### Scenario: "What does your traction look like?"

**Direct version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Here's the snapshot:
>
> - [$X] MRR / ARR, growing [Y]% MoM (last 6 months)
> - [N] paying customers, [Z]% net revenue retention
> - [Logo or two if shareable]
> - [LTV/CAC or payback metric if it's good]
>
> Happy to share the model on a call.
>
> [First name]

**Warmer version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Good question, here's where we are:
>
> Currently at $X MRR, up from $Y six months ago. We've been growing about Z% MoM for the last 6 months, and the curve has been pretty consistent which has been the surprise.
>
> [N] paying customers across [segment description]. [Logo or two if shareable]. Retention's been the strongest signal, [%] NRR, with the top quartile of customers expanding ARR by [%] in the first 6 months.
>
> If it's helpful, happy to walk through the financial model and the cohort math on a call. The numbers tell a cleaner story when you can see the cohorts side by side.
>
> [First name]

### Scenario: "What are you raising at? Valuation?"

**No lead yet - direct version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> We're letting the round price itself. Talking to a few potential leads, will share specifics with whoever's most interested in leading.
>
> Range we're seeing in similar deals at our stage and metrics is [$X-Y post]. Happy to dig into our thinking on a call.
>
> [First name]

**No lead - warmer version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Honestly, we're holding off on a hard number until we have a lead in place. Have a few conversations going with potential leads, and I'd rather let one of them set the price than anchor either way myself.
>
> For context, deals we're looking at as comps are landing in the [$X-Y post] range. But that's a starting point, not a number I'm wedded to.
>
> Worth a call to talk through the round structure and our thinking?
>
> [First name]

**With a lead - direct version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> [Lead Firm] is leading at [$X post] on a [structure: priced equity / SAFE / convertible]. Closing in [timing].
>
> [$Y] of the [$Z] round committed, looking to fill out the remaining [$W] with strategic checks. Min check we're taking is [$amount].
>
> Happy to share the term sheet and discuss on a call.
>
> [First name]

### Scenario: "Who else is in the round?"

**Direct version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> [Lead Firm if applicable] is leading. Following on are [Firm A], [Firm B], [Angel C with relevant background], [Angel D]. Total committed: [$X] of [$Y].
>
> Happy to make intros to any of them as references.
>
> [First name]

**Warmer version (when commitments are thin):**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Honest answer: it's still early. We're in active conversations with [N] potential leads, and have soft circles from [N] angels for [$X] of the [$Y] round. You'd be one of the first to commit if you decide to move.
>
> Strategy-wise, we're not racing to fill the round. Want the right partners more than a fast close.
>
> Happy to share where things stand on a call.
>
> [First name]

### Scenario: "Why now? Why not wait until your Series A?"

**Direct version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Three reasons, fast:
>
> 1. The price moves. At [$X post] now vs. likely [$3-5X] at the A, your ownership for the same dollars is [N]x.
> 2. Conviction premium. The investors who back us at [seed/A] get the upside. The ones who pile on at the next round pay the multiple.
> 3. What you unlock now: intros to [target customer segment], help recruiting [key role], thesis-shaping. That's worth more pre-product-market fit than post.
>
> Happy to talk through the math.
>
> [First name]

**Warmer version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Fair question. The case I'd make:
>
> The price now is [$X post]. By the Series A, even if we just keep doing what we're doing, we're likely at [$3-5X] minimum. So the same check buys you [N]x the ownership today.
>
> Beyond price, the early backers shape the company in ways the later ones don't. We're going to need help with [specific challenge, e.g., enterprise sales, regulated procurement, recruiting a head of growth]. That's exactly where your [Firm/portfolio/expertise] adds the most.
>
> Investors who wait for the A pay the multiple but skip the influence. Most of the best LPs in your portfolio companies probably wish they'd been in earlier.
>
> Worth a call to walk through it?
>
> [First name]

### Scenario: "Pass - not the right fit for us right now"

**Direct version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Appreciate the clear answer, that's actually rare. Genuinely useful.
>
> One ask: anyone in your network who'd be a better fit for [stage/sector]? One intro would mean a lot.
>
> Either way, I'll send an update in 60-90 days when we've got more to share. All the best.
>
> [First name]

**Warmer version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Thanks for the candor, and for taking the time to look at it. A clear pass is more useful than three weeks of silence, so genuinely appreciate it.
>
> If you have one second, is there anyone in your network who you think would actually be a fit for [stage/sector]? One warm intro from you would carry more than ten of my cold emails.
>
> Either way, I'll close the loop with an update in 60-90 days. Hopefully we'll have a chance to work together on the next one.
>
> [First name]

### Scenario: "Send me your data room / DD package"

**Direct version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Sending tomorrow. Here's what'll be in it:
>
> - Cap table (current and pro-forma)
> - 3-year model (monthly)
> - Customer list and revenue breakdown
> - Cohort retention data (12 months)
> - Founder bios, IP assignments, key contracts
>
> [Anything that's still pending:] [SOC 2 letter - auditor engaged, expected November] [or similar].
>
> Will share access via [DocSend / Visible / Quoroom].
>
> [First name]

**Warmer version:**

> Hi [first name],
>
> Great, I'll get the data room over to you tomorrow. Quick note on what's in it and what's not:
>
> Ready to share: cap table (current and pro-forma post-round), our 3-year financial model, full customer list with revenue breakdown, 12 months of cohort retention data, founder bios, IP assignments, top customer contracts.
>
> What's still in progress: SOC 2 Type 1 (we engaged [auditor] in [month], targeting completion in November). Wanted to flag that upfront rather than have you wonder.
>
> Access will come through [DocSend/Visible/Quoroom] so I can see who's looking at what. Helps us know when you're moving and when you're stuck.
>
> [First name]

### Scenario: Investor goes quiet mid-process

> Hi [first name],
>
> Hope all's well. Been a couple of weeks since [last meeting / last email], wanted to check in.
>
> Quick update from our side: [one new data point - closed customer, hire, metric movement, big partnership].
>
> Where things stand on the round: [$X] of [$Y] committed, targeting close [date]. We have [N] other firms in active diligence.
>
> If [Firm] is still considering, what's the next step on your side and what's the timing? If it's not a fit, total honesty would help me focus elsewhere.
>
> [First name]

**Why this works:**
- Doesn't accuse them of ghosting.
- Adds new info that gives them a reason to re-engage.
- States the round is moving (creates real urgency, not fake FOMO).
- Asks directly: yes, no, or what do you need? Easier for them to answer than another "checking in" note.

## 5. Post-meeting recap patterns (Mode 7)

The 24-hour recap is one of the highest-leverage emails in any fundraise. Investors form their lasting impression of a founder partly from the recap. Most founders send "great to meet you, looking forward to next steps" and waste the moment. The ones who send a real recap stand out immediately.

Hard rules across all three patterns:
- 50-120 words
- Reply in the same email thread, no new subject line
- No "great to meet you" opening, no "thanks again for your time" closing
- Every commitment the investor made is named with timing
- Every ask the investor made is answered with a date
- Tone calibrates to the founder's read of the room

### Pattern 1: Warm meeting recap

Investor was engaged, asked specific questions, made commitments. Lead with confidence. Reference one specific moment from the call to prove you were listening, then the substance.

> Hi [first_name],
>
> That point you made on [specific moment from the call, e.g., "the integration overhead being the real moat in mid-market"] has been rattling around since we hung up.
>
> Quick recap:
> - [Topic 1, one line]
> - [Topic 2, one line]
> - [Topic 3, one line, optional]
>
> On [investor's commitment, e.g., "the partnership share"]: [confirmed with timing, e.g., "clean one-pager in your inbox Wed EOD."]
> On [investor's ask 1, e.g., "data room access"]: [confirmed with timing, e.g., "link Tue morning, scoped to you, add another partner if useful."]
> On [investor's ask 2, e.g., "the customer reference"]: [confirmed with timing.]
>
> Anything else useful before [the next concrete step]?
>
> [First name]

Filled, this lands at ~85-100 words.

### Pattern 2: Lukewarm meeting recap

Investor surfaced specific concerns or seemed measured. Address the concerns head-on. Don't pretend they didn't come up. If you have one piece of new info or context that wasn't in the meeting, share it. Soften the next step into something low-commitment.

> Hi [first_name],
>
> Thanks for the direct pushback on [specific concern raised, e.g., "competitor density in the AI ops space"]. Worth answering straight on.
>
> - On [concern 1]: [one-line direct response with new info or context]
> - On [concern 2]: [one-line direct response]
> - On the [topic] the partnership wanted to look at: [your read on whether you can address it now or whether it's a Q1+ question]
>
> >>> NEW INFO (optional): one piece of progress or context that was not in the meeting, shared briefly. e.g., "Two more pilots came online this morning that change the concentration story you flagged." Skip if you don't have something real <<<
>
> Happy to send a short written response to the partnership if it'd help the conversation there. Otherwise I'll check back in two weeks.
>
> [First name]

Filled, ~110-120 words. Right at the cap.

### Pattern 3: Cold or hard-to-tell recap

Investor was polite but the energy was off. Shorter and cleaner. No momentum-claiming language. Just commitments and asks with dates. Make it easy for them to disengage gracefully or surprise you with a yes.

> Hi [first_name],
>
> Recap and the items I owe you:
>
> - [Topic 1, one line]
> - [Topic 2, one line]
>
> On [specific commitment investor made, if any]: noted, will keep an eye out.
> On [specific ask investor had]: sending [X] by [date].
>
> Available if any follow-up questions come up.
>
> [First name]

Filled, ~50-60 words. Hits the floor of the 50-120 range.

## 6. Pipeline stall recovery patterns (Mode 8)

When an investor was in active conversation, took a meeting, said positive things, then went silent for 14+ days. Two scripts. The skill defaults to Script A. Script B is only drafted when the founder has real, verifiable competitive pressure (see refusal logic below).

### Script A: Soft re-engage (default)

Three variants for the three most common stall points.

**Variant A1: Silence after a partner meeting (no commitment to next step)**

> Hi [first_name],
>
> Quick update: [one new data point. Real, not invented. e.g., "closed our second top-25 customer this week, brings ARR over $X."]
>
> Last we talked, you were going to circle back internally on next steps after our 1:1. If timing's shifted on [Firm]'s side, totally understand.
>
> If still active, happy to push the next step on your schedule. Either way, would help to know.
>
> [First name]

**Variant A2: Silence after the partner agreed to share with the partnership**

> Hi [first_name],
>
> Quick update: [one new data point].
>
> When we last spoke, you were planning to bring it to the partnership for [specific Monday / Friday / time-named]. If the partnership share happened and the answer is "not for us," totally fine, just want to know so I can plan.
>
> If still in process, happy to provide anything else useful before the discussion.
>
> [First name]

**Variant A3: Silence after reference checks were supposed to start**

> Hi [first_name],
>
> Wanted to check in. We had [N] customers lined up to take your reference calls (including [Customer 1]) and they've been holding their schedules open for [N] weeks now.
>
> If the diligence is paused, I want to release them back. If still moving, happy to set up the calls this week.
>
> Either way, would help to know where things stand.
>
> [First name]

Each lands at ~50-70 words. Restate the specific stall point, add one new piece of info or context where natural, plain ask: still in or out.

### Script B: Force a decision (only with verifiable real pressure)

Two variants depending on the timeline of the competing pressure.

**Variant B1: Pressure where timeline is days (term sheet imminent)**

> Hi [first_name],
>
> Wanted to flag where things stand on our side, since we last talked [X weeks ago].
>
> Another lead is on track to send a term sheet this week. If [Firm] is still in active conversation, I want to find a way to keep you in. If timing's shifted, no hard feelings, but I'd need to know by [specific day, 2-3 business days out] to plan around it.
>
> Happy to share what I can about the other process if it helps your internal discussion.
>
> [First name]

**Variant B2: Pressure where timeline is weeks (round closing date)**

> Hi [first_name],
>
> Wanted to flag round timing on our side, since we last talked [X weeks ago].
>
> We're targeting a hard close on [specific date, 3-4 weeks out]. Lead is in place. Room for one more institutional check at [size] alongside.
>
> If [Firm] is still considering, I'd want to know whether to hold a slot. Happy to walk through the term sheet and the model on a call if that would help the decision.
>
> If timing's shifted on your side, no hard feelings.
>
> [First name]

### When the skill refuses to draft Script B

Script B only works when the pressure is real and verifiable. The skill refuses to draft it without these conditions met. The refusal is not a soft suggestion; it is the right call for the founder's long-term reputation.

**Counts as real pressure:**
- Another investor has explicitly committed to send a term sheet with a named timeline (this week, by Friday, by [specific date])
- A lead is in place and the round has a real close date the founder intends to honor regardless
- A separate strategic process (acquisition LOI, partnership term sheet, customer-funded round) is genuinely moving on a tight clock

**Does not count as real pressure:**
- Generalized "interest from other firms" without a specific commitment or timeline
- A self-imposed close date with no anchoring lead or term sheet
- A "fast close" the founder hopes to manufacture by sending Script B
- Early-stage conversations with other firms that have not progressed past first meetings

**What to say to the founder when refusing:**

> "I won't draft Script B for this scenario. The pressure you described is [specific reason: not yet committed / no term sheet / self-imposed]. If the partner here calls the other firm or another partner who knows the other firm, your reputation in the network suffers, and investors in a sector talk to each other constantly. Script A is the right move here. If the pressure becomes real later (term sheet committed, hard close date), come back and I'll draft B."

**Why fabricated tension burns long-term:**

The founder is not operating in a one-shot game. They are building a reputation across a network of partners who share notes. A fabricated "another lead is moving" claim that turns out to be soft gets remembered the next time the founder raises, often by the same partners or their peers. The cost of getting caught once is years of harder fundraises.

Script A preserves optionality. Script B, used honestly, accelerates a real decision. Script B, used falsely, destroys reputation. The math: the upside of a faked Script B is one investor's faster decision; the downside is reputational damage across the partner network for years. Always Script A unless the pressure is verifiable.

If the founder pushes back ("but the urgency would help me close this"), the answer is the same. Real urgency works because the pressure is verifiable. Fake urgency becomes evident inside two phone calls.

## Final notes on every output

- **Always sign with first name only.** Full names in signatures look corporate.
- **Always include the trackable deck link** (DocSend, Visible, Quoroom, Pitch). Never PDF attachment.
- **Always send from the company domain.** firstname@company.com. Not gmail.
- **Always one ask per email.** Specific. Easy to say yes to.
- **Match the investor's register.** If they wrote 2 lines, write 2 lines. If they wrote a paragraph, the warmer version probably fits.
