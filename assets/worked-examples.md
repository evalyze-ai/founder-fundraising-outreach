# Worked Examples: Before / After

Real-shape founder emails (names, numbers, and details fictionalized) showing the kind of transformations this skill produces. Use these to internalize the pattern.

**Note (v6):** some examples predate the current rules. Where any example conflicts with SKILL.md or `references/anti-ai-tells.md`, the v6 rules win, specifically: cold first touch is 40-70 words (80 cap) with ZERO links, attachments, or calendar links; exactly ONE proof point (no stacked metric strings); at most one recipient detail; four-block shape with blank lines; subject under 55 chars with no colons or dashes; optional compound hyphens dropped; follow-ups never open with "following up" or "circling back." Internalize the strategic moves here, not the exact skeletons. Example 1 below has been updated to the v6 shape.

## Example 1: B2B SaaS cold email

### Before

> Subject: Investment opportunity in revolutionary AI platform for healthcare
>
> Dear Mr. Patel,
>
> I hope this email finds you well. My name is Mike Chen, and I am the CEO and Co-Founder of Quill Inc. I have been a long-time admirer of Greylock and your incredible portfolio of disruptive companies in the healthcare and enterprise space.
>
> Quill is a revolutionary, AI-powered, end-to-end operations platform that is reimagining the way mid-market hospitals operate. We are leveraging cutting-edge artificial intelligence to unlock significant value for our customers and democratize access to enterprise-grade tooling that was previously only available to the largest health systems.
>
> Our innovative solution has been seeing strong growth and traction. We have signed up multiple customers across the country and have generated significant revenue in a short period of time. We believe Quill is positioned to be a true game-changer in the $50B+ healthcare operations market and could become the dominant platform in this rapidly growing vertical.
>
> We are currently raising our seed round and would love to leverage your expertise and network to help us scale. Would you be open to a quick chat sometime in the coming weeks to discuss this exciting opportunity? I have attached our pitch deck for your review.
>
> Looking forward to hearing back from you soon.
>
> Best regards,
> Michael Chen
> CEO and Co-Founder, Quill Inc.

**Word count: 218**

### After

> Subject: hospital ops software, 11 hospitals paying
>
> Hi Sarah,
>
> I ran operations at a 300 bed hospital for six years before starting Quill. We replace the six tools mid-market hospitals juggle for ops with one workspace. 11 hospitals pay for it now and 92% have stayed.
>
> Your vertical AI post last week matched what we see in the field.
>
> Raising our seed. Worth 20 minutes next week?
>
> Mike

**Word count: 64**

### What changed and why

- **Subject line:** "Investment opportunity in revolutionary AI platform for healthcare" said nothing useful. The new subject is six plain words with one number, under 55 characters, no colon or dash, and could only be this company.
- **First line:** "I hope this email finds you well" is filler, and flattery about Greylock is worse. The replacement is the founder's checkable identity, which no tool writes unless it's true.
- **Buzzword density:** "revolutionary," "AI-powered," "end-to-end," "reimagining," "leveraging cutting-edge," "unlock significant value," "democratize," "innovative solution," "strong growth," "game-changer," "rapidly growing." Every one cut. Replaced with concrete nouns and one number.
- **Traction:** "Strong growth and traction" said nothing. The tempting overcorrection is four metrics in one line, which reads machine-compressed. One proof point (11 paying, 92% stayed) carries it; MRR and growth rate are held back for follow-up 1 and the deck.
- **Personalization:** one line about Sarah's post, then move on. Fit, not flattery.
- **Ask:** "Quick chat sometime in the coming weeks" → "Worth 20 minutes next week?" Seven words, question mark, numerals.
- **Clickables:** attachment removed and NOT replaced with a link. Zero links in a first touch: better deliverability, better engagement (asking beats dropping the deck), better read. The trackable deck link goes out when Sarah replies.
- **Shape:** four blocks with blank lines between. 184 words → 64.
- **Sign-off:** "Best regards, Michael Chen, CEO and Co-Founder, Quill Inc." → "Mike."

## Example 2: AI consumer pre-seed

### Before

> Subject: Mira - Voice AI Journaling App - Seeking Pre-Seed Funding
>
> Hi James,
>
> I'm reaching out because I'm building Mira, an AI-powered voice journaling app that is looking to revolutionize the way people reflect on their lives. With AI being such a hot topic and the journaling market growing, we believe Mira is uniquely positioned to disrupt the wellness space.
>
> About me: I'm a solo founder with 3 years of experience at Snap, where I worked on voice products. I have a deep passion for consumer AI and mental wellness, and I believe that with the right partners, Mira can become the leading platform in this category.
>
> So far, we have built up an impressive waitlist of over 50,000 users and we are launching our beta in November. We're really excited about the early signal we're seeing.
>
> I'd love to set up a 30-minute call to share more about what we're building and explore if there's mutual interest. I've attached our deck for your review.
>
> Best,
> Priya

**Word count: 173**

### After

> Subject: Voice journaling for 200M people who hate writing, 50K waitlist
>
> Subject: voice journaling, 50K waitlist in six weeks
>
> Hi James,
>
> I spent three years at Snap shipping voice features, now I'm building Mira, voice first journaling. You talk, it gives you back a structured reflection. 50K people joined the waitlist in six weeks, all organic.
>
> Your essay on consumer AI being a UX problem is half our thesis.
>
> Raising $1M pre-seed. Open to 20 minutes?
>
> Priya

**Word count: 58**

### What changed and why

- **Subject line:** Before was generic "[Company] - [What it is] - [Stage]" with dashes. After is plain words, one number, commas only, under 55 chars.
- **Opener:** "Reaching out because I'm building..." is throat-clearing. Replaced with the founder's checkable identity (three years at Snap on voice), which is the strongest card a pre-revenue founder holds.
- **Description:** "Revolutionize the way people reflect" is empty. Replaced with what Mira actually does in two plain clauses. "Voice first" with no hyphen, typed-fast register.
- **One proof point:** the waitlist. The beta date and the $400K in angel commits are deliberately held back, they're follow-up 1 and follow-up 2 material now.
- **One recipient detail:** James's essay, one line ("is half our thesis"), then done. The old version spent a full flattering paragraph on it.
- **Ask:** "30-minute call to explore mutual interest" → "Open to 20 minutes?" Numerals, question mark.
- **Clickables:** zero. No Calendly, no deck. Both go out when James replies.

## Example 3: Marketplace Series A follow-up #2

**Context:** Founder sent a cold email 10 days ago. Sent follow-up #1 three days ago. No reply. This is follow-up #2.

### Before

> Subject: Re: Following up on my email about Trellis
>
> Hi David,
>
> I wanted to follow up on my previous email since I haven't heard back yet. I understand you're busy but wanted to make sure my email didn't get lost in your inbox.
>
> As a quick reminder, Trellis is a B2B marketplace for industrial parts. We're seeing strong growth and we believe we'd be a great fit for your fund's thesis on vertical marketplaces.
>
> If you have a chance, I'd really appreciate any feedback or interest. Happy to chat anytime.
>
> Best,
> Sasha

**Word count: 92**

### After (in the same thread, no new subject)

> Hi David,
>
> Quick update: spent last week with three procurement leads at mid market manufacturers. All of them run 80 plus supplier relationships out of spreadsheets, and the pain is reorder cadence, not price discovery. That gap is our wedge. GMV crossed $220K last week.
>
> Worth 20 minutes, Tue or Thu?
>
> Sasha

**Word count: 52**

### What changed and why

- **Opening:** "I wanted to follow up since I haven't heard back" is a guilt-trip and adds nothing. Cut. Replaced with substantive new content that's interesting on its own merit.
- **Customer insight:** The "three procurement leads, 80+ supplier relationships in spreadsheets" detail is specific enough that David might forward it to a portfolio company. That's the test of a good follow-up: would a partner forward it?
- **Metric update:** "$220K monthly GMV (up from $200K)" shows momentum since the original email. This is the move: every follow-up should add a real data point.
- **Ask:** "Happy to chat anytime" → "25 min anytime, Tue or Thu next week."
- **No "as a reminder":** David remembers or he doesn't. Reminding him just costs words.

## Example 4: Reply to "Send me your deck"

**Context:** Founder sent a cold email. Investor replied "Send me your deck" in 5 words.

### Direct version (matches investor's terse register)

> Hi Lin,
>
> Here's the deck: [trackable link]
>
> Quick teaser: Salt builds the data layer for restaurant inventory. $80K MRR across 240 restaurants, 95% retention. Slides 4 and 9 are the main story.
>
> 25 min next Tue or Wed if you want to walk through it.
>
> Anya

### Warmer version (use if Lin wrote a warmer cold-email reply)

> Hi Lin,
>
> Thanks for taking a look. Here's the deck: [trackable link]
>
> Quick context before you click through: Salt is the data layer for restaurant inventory. The thing I'd flag is slide 9, where the cohort retention curve sits. That's the chart that surprised me most when we ran it, top quartile of restaurants are using us 4x as much by month 6.
>
> Currently $80K MRR across 240 restaurants, 95% retention.
>
> Happy to walk through it on a call any day next week other than Friday. Otherwise, all good if you'd rather review and come back with questions.
>
> Anya

### What's happening here

- The investor wrote 5 words. The "direct" version matches that energy.
- The "warmer" version is for a relationship-building moment, when the investor's reply was longer or the founder wants to seed a conversation.
- Both pre-frame the deck (slides 4 and 9, the cohort curve). This dramatically improves the chance the investor actually reads the deck instead of opening it for 30 seconds.
- Both include the ask without being needy.

## Example 5: Polite pass reply

**Context:** Investor wrote "Sorry, not the right stage for us right now, but please keep us posted as you grow."

### Direct version

> Hi Marcus,
>
> Appreciate the clear answer, that's actually rare. Genuinely useful.
>
> One ask: anyone in your network who'd be a fit for our seed (US-based, fintech-focused, $250K-$1M check)? One intro would mean a lot.
>
> Either way, I'll send an update in 60-90 days when we've got more to share. All the best.
>
> Sasha

### Warmer version

> Hi Marcus,
>
> Thanks for the candor, and for taking the time to look at it. A clear pass is more useful than three weeks of silence, so genuinely appreciate it.
>
> If you have one second, is there anyone in your network you think would actually be a fit for our seed (US-based, fintech-focused, $250K-$1M check)? One warm intro from you would carry more than ten cold emails from me.
>
> Either way, I'll close the loop with an update in 60-90 days. Hopefully we'll have a chance to work together on the next one.
>
> Sasha

### What's happening here

- No begging, no whining, no "are you sure?"
- The thank-you is genuine but brief.
- Asks for ONE referral, not "introductions to your network."
- The referral request is qualified: "US-based, fintech-focused, $250K-$1M check." That makes it 10x easier for Marcus to think of the right person.
- Sets up the 60-day update clearly. This is the move: the first pass is rarely permanent. Most founders forget this and disappear after a no.

## Example 6: Founder-pasted bad draft → rewritten

**Context:** Founder pastes a 400-word email full of buzzwords and asks Claude to make it better.

### What the founder pasted

> Subject: Disrupting the future of work with AI
>
> Dear Investor,
>
> I hope this message finds you well. My name is David Park, and I'm the founder & CEO of Helix, a next-generation, AI-powered platform that is revolutionizing how knowledge workers operate at scale. We are at the cutting edge of a paradigm shift in productivity, and we believe Helix has the potential to become a true game-changer in the future-of-work space.
>
> Our world-class team consists of myself (formerly an engineer at Google, with deep expertise in machine learning) and my co-founder Anjali, who brings best-in-class product chops from her time at Asana. Together, we are building a holistic, end-to-end ecosystem that empowers teams to seamlessly leverage AI to unlock unprecedented productivity gains.
>
> The market for our platform is enormous. The total addressable market for productivity software is over $100B, and we believe we are uniquely positioned to capture a significant share of this rapidly growing vertical. We have already generated significant interest from beta users and have an impressive pipeline of enterprise customers in active discussions.
>
> We are currently raising our seed round of $2M to supercharge our growth and accelerate our roadmap. We have soft commitments from a strong group of angel investors and would love to bring on a strategic VC partner who can help us scale. Would you be open to a quick coffee chat to learn more about this exciting opportunity?
>
> Please find our pitch deck attached for your review. Looking forward to hearing back!
>
> Best,
> David Park
> Founder & CEO, Helix

**Word count: 261**

### Diagnosis

- Subject line: "Disrupting the future of work with AI" tells the investor nothing about what Helix actually is or does. And "disrupting" is a banned word.
- First sentence is about the founder, not the investor. There's no personalization at all, this email could be sent to any VC.
- "Next-generation, AI-powered platform that is revolutionizing how knowledge workers operate at scale" is a buzzword sandwich with no actual product description.
- Team section is too long and uses "world-class" and "best-in-class" without proof.
- "Holistic, end-to-end ecosystem that empowers teams to seamlessly leverage AI to unlock unprecedented productivity gains" is meaningless.
- TAM claim ($100B) is unsupported and is one of the strongest anti-signals in cold emails.
- Traction is vague: "significant interest from beta users," "impressive pipeline." No numbers.
- Round info is light: "$2M seed" but no commitments named, no use-of-funds, no target close.
- Ask is "quick coffee chat," which signals "I have nothing specific to say."
- PDF attachment.
- Sign-off includes "Founder & CEO" which is corporate.

### Rewrite (assuming the founder filled in real specifics, flagging the gaps)

> Subject: legal ops AI, 14 firms paying
>
> Hi >>> PARTNER_FIRST_NAME: fill in, do not send to a generic inbox <<<,
>
> >>> HOOK: one line referencing this partner's recent post, podcast, or investment. Skip the email entirely if you can't find one <<<
>
> Building Helix, AI that drafts first pass memos from case files for mid market law firms. 14 firms pay for it and 91% have stayed.
>
> Raising $2M seed. Worth 20 minutes next week?
>
> David

**Word count: ~55 with hook filled**

### What changed and why

- **Subject line:** the actual wedge plus one number, six words, no colons or dashes.
- **TAM claim:** Removed entirely. A $100B TAM with no math is anti-signal. The proof point does the work the TAM was trying to do.
- **Team section:** Cut. The team line can come later, in the call.
- **Buzzwords:** Eliminated. "Disrupting," "next-generation," "AI-powered," "revolutionizing," "paradigm shift," "game-changer," "world-class," "best-in-class," "holistic," "end-to-end," "ecosystem," "empowers," "seamlessly leverage," "unlock unprecedented," "supercharge." Every one removed.
- **One proof point:** 14 paying firms and their retention. MRR, the AmLaw pilots, and the soft-circle are all held back for follow-ups and the deck, where they'll each get their own moment.
- **Hook:** Flagged as a hard gap with a visible placeholder. Without a specific signal from the partner, the email doesn't send.
- **Ask:** "Quick coffee chat" → "Worth 20 minutes next week?"
- **Clickables:** Calendly and deck link both removed. Nothing to click in a first touch; the trackable deck goes out on reply.
- **Length:** 261 words → ~55.

### What to customize before sending

- Fill both placeholders. Do not send without the hook.
- Verify the traction numbers are current (these were inferred from David's draft, double-check).
- Have the trackable deck (DocSend, Visible, etc.) ready to send within the hour of a reply.
- Send from david@helix.com, not Gmail, at the partner's local morning.

## What to take from these examples

The pattern across all six is the same:

1. **Cut everything that isn't earning its place.** Most founder drafts are 2x too long.
2. **Replace adjectives with numbers.** Always.
3. **Make the personalization specific to this partner**, not the firm or the portfolio in general.
4. **Have one ask, and make it easy to say yes to.**
5. **Sound like a person, not a press release.**

Apply these to every output and the founder's reply rate will jump.
